Proposed research direction

Current research proposal · 2025

Orchestrating multi-technology sustainability transitions in emerging economies: an agent-based open innovation framework for Indonesia

The problem

Two technologies, one transition

Indonesia is simultaneously transitioning across electric vehicles and decentralised microgrid energy — two systems that are mutually dependent yet governed separately. EV charging creates anchor demand that makes microgrids viable; microgrid reliability reduces EV range anxiety. Yet neither technology's literature has modelled their co-evolution in an emerging economy context.

The contribution

Transition orchestration capacity

This study introduces the concept of transition orchestration capacity — the institutional capability to coordinate across simultaneous technology transitions. Using agent-based modelling calibrated to Indonesian empirical data, it tests how different policy sequencing strategies produce divergent co-evolution pathways, generating findings that single-technology models structurally cannot.

The method

Agent-based modelling

A spatially-explicit ABM built in Mesa (Python) simulates five agent types across urban, peri-urban, and rural Indonesian patches. Household decision rules are calibrated from prior survey data. Developer rules use NPV threshold logic. Government agents operate across three institutional layers with realistic policy lags and budget constraints.

The output

Three-paper programme

A conceptual framework paper establishing the co-evolution argument; a simulation methodology paper presenting the ABM; and a policy scenario paper comparing reactive, proactive, and technology-specific government intervention strategies. Target journals: Technological Forecasting & Social Change, Journal of Open Innovation, and Transportation Research Interdisciplinary Perspectives.

The three interaction loops

1
Viability loop
EV adoption raises microgrid load viability → microgrid investment → charging reliability improves → EV adoption accelerates. The reinforcing loop that, if it kicks in, drives the transition.
2
Tariff squeeze loop
EV load growth stresses PLN grid → tariff increases → EV cost rises OR microgrid becomes cheaper → investment redirected. A balancing loop that either dampens or redirects the transition.
3
Spatial mismatch loop
EV subsidies concentrate adoption in high-income urban areas → EV-microgrid co-evolution happens only in cities → rural and peri-urban areas are left behind. The equity dimension of the transition.

Agent population

Household / consumer
Primary adopter · TPB-based decision rule
Makes two coupled decisions each tick: whether to adopt an EV (driven by attitude, social norms, perceived behavioural control) and whether to connect to a microgrid (driven by reliability gain vs. premium tariff tradeoff). Calibrated from Bandung and Jakarta survey data.
Microgrid developer
Supply-side investor · NPV threshold rule
Invests when projected EV + baseline load exceeds minimum viable load threshold and NPV clears a hurdle rate. Applies institutional risk discounts for PLN tariff volatility. Enters lobby state when financially marginal, signalling upward to government.
Government / regulator
Three-layer orchestrator · Trigger-based rule
Operates as national (ESDM/KEMENPERIN), regional (Pemda), and PLN layers at different speeds. Monitors EV rate, microgrid coverage, equity gap, and lobby pressure. Deploys demand-side, supply-side, or regulatory interventions under budget constraint.
EV infrastructure investor
Charger deployment · ROI-based rule
Deploys charging infrastructure based on EV density, electricity tariff stability, and grid reliability in a patch. In low-reliability areas, adds a premium for microgrid-backed charging, which feeds back into household adoption costs.
Electricity utility (PLN)
Grid operator · Load-response tariff rule
Sets tariffs dynamically in response to load growth. When EV demand spikes without grid expansion, raises tariffs or imposes load-shedding — driving households and developers toward microgrid alternatives. Creates the tariff squeeze loop.
Peer influence network
Social overlay · Word-of-mouth diffusion
A social graph connecting household agents. Adoption by network neighbours increases EV adoption probability. Satisfied adopters contribute a positive signal; dissatisfied adopters contribute a negative signal, reproducing cluster diffusion patterns.
