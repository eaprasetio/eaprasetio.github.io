Research Overview

I am a faculty member and researcher at SBM-ITB, where I led the Management of Technology Laboratory (MoT Lab) and currently serve as Vice Dean for Academic Affairs. My doctoral research from Friedrich-Schiller-Universität Jena (2017) focused on disruptive innovation and network effects, and my work since then has expanded to cover a wide spectrum of technology management, innovation ecosystems, and sustainable mobility.

My research is empirically grounded in the Indonesian context — addressing technology diffusion, EV policy, autonomous vehicle adoption, digital banking transformation, and university spin-off commercialization — while contributing to globally relevant theoretical frameworks. I regularly publish in high-impact Scopus-indexed journals including Technological Forecasting & Social Change, Transportation Research Interdisciplinary Perspectives, Journal of Open Innovation, and IATSS Research.

Beyond academia, I maintain extensive engagement with industry and government, advising on Industry 4.0 readiness assessments, EV infrastructure policy, digital talent readiness, and ESG development for major Indonesian corporations and ministries.

Co-Evolution of Electric Vehicles and Microgrids
An Agent-Based Modelling Framework for Sustainability Transition Orchestration in Indonesia

Indonesia faces two pressing transitions simultaneously: scaling electric vehicle (EV) adoption to decarbonise transport, and expanding microgrid deployment to reach the ~25 million citizens without reliable electricity. These two challenges are not independent — they are structurally coupled. EV adoption creates anchor demand that can make microgrids financially viable; microgrid deployment reduces range anxiety that constrains EV adoption. Yet policy and research have treated them as entirely separate problems. This research aims to change that.

🔄 The Viability Loop

EV adoption → microgrid load viability → developer investment → charging reliability → more EV adoption. When activated, this reinforcing loop drives the transition forward.

⚖️ The Tariff Squeeze

EV load growth → PLN grid stress → tariff increase → adoption slows — unless microgrid deployment breaks the cycle by enabling households to bypass grid load increases.

📍 The Spatial Mismatch

Demand-side EV subsidies without microgrid support concentrate adoption in high-income urban areas — widening the energy access gap between cities and peri-urban/rural communities.

The Approach

A spatially-explicit Agent-Based Model (ABM) built in Mesa/Python, simulating five interacting agent types — households, microgrid developers, EV infrastructure investors, government layers, and PLN — calibrated to Indonesian empirical data from surveys, PLN regional statistics, and field interviews across three regions.

The Theoretical Contribution

The research introduces Transition Orchestration Capacity (TOC) — the institutional capability to sequence, target, and coordinate policy interventions across co-evolving technology systems under budget constraint and institutional lag. TOC extends the Multi-Level Perspective toward multi-technology emerging economy contexts.

Opinion & Public Writing
Ha-Joon Chang dan Kemunafikan Negara Maju
Indonesia, Danantara, dan Jalan Panjang Menuju Industrialisasi yang Mandiri
✍ Eko Agus Prasetio📅 31 Oktober 2025🏛 Dosen SBM-ITB

Negara-negara maju sering menasihati negara berkembang agar mempercayai pasar bebas. Namun, seperti diungkap Ha-Joon Chang, nasihat itu menyembunyikan paradoks besar: justru proteksionisme dan kebijakan industri yang membuat mereka maju — sebuah fenomena yang disebutnya kicking the ladder.

Industrial PolicyHa-Joon ChangDanantaraIndustrialisasi Indonesia

Chang memberikan data dan analisis historis yang menunjukkan bahwa Indonesia — meski termasuk yang tumbuh cepat sejak 1947 — belum upgraded menjadi negara maju seperti Korea Selatan. Pertumbuhan Indonesia masih sangat bergantung pada komoditas (extractive-based economy), dan porsi industri manufaktur terhadap PDB terus menurun: dari sekitar 28 persen pada awal 2000-an menjadi di bawah 18 persen pada 2023 — sebuah gejala premature de-industrialization.

Chang menganjurkan transformative industrial policy: dari "getting out of the way" menjadi "helping business succeed", dengan visi jangka panjang, dialog aktif antara pemerintah dan industri, serta pembelajaran dan koreksi berkelanjutan. Namun, Dani Rodrik mengingatkan bahwa kebijakan semacam ini hanya efektif dalam konteks institusi yang kuat.

Dalam konteks ini, Danantara — lembaga superholding investasi negara — menjadi ujian besar. Jika dikelola dengan tata kelola yang kuat dan transparan, ia bisa menjadi contoh nyata dari transformative industrial policy. Tanpa institusi yang tangguh, ia mudah tergelincir menjadi kapitalisme kroni.

Catatan: ide dan draf tulisan saya buat, kemudian disempurnakan dengan bantuan AI agar lebih mudah dibaca.

🔒 Cybersecurity & Safety Autonomous Vehicles Scopus Q1 · 2023

Evaluating Perceived Safety of Autonomous Vehicle: The Influence of Privacy and Cybersecurity to Cognitive and Emotional Safety

Eko Agus Prasetio, Cintia Nurliyana

IATSS Research · Elsevier · 47(2), 160–170

Autonomous vehicles promise safer roads and smoother traffic — but what happens when a self-driving car gets hacked? This study investigates how cybersecurity and privacy concerns shape whether people feel safe enough to actually use AVs, revealing a critical and often overlooked barrier to adoption.
🤔
The Problem

AVs are vulnerable to hacking and data exploitation. While safety research often focuses on crash prevention, this study asks a different question: do people feel safe — and what drives that feeling?

📋
The Study Design

466 participants responded to a survey testing a comprehensive perceived safety model. Responses were analysed using Structural Equation Modelling (SEM) — capturing both direct and causal relationships between variables.

🏆
The #1 Finding

Privacy cybersecurity is the most powerful predictor of perceived safety — outranking both emotional and cognitive safety. If people don't trust that their data is safe, they won't feel safe in an AV, full stop.

📊
Who Will Adopt?

31.1% of participants expressed intent to use a Level 2 AV (partial automation), while just 8.8% were willing to go to Level 5 (full autonomy). Higher autonomy = higher cybersecurity anxiety.

🧠
Cognitive vs Emotional Safety

The study separates cognitive safety (rational assessment of AV reliability) from emotional safety (feelings of comfort and reassurance). Cybersecurity concerns damage both — but hit emotional safety hardest.

🏛️
Policy Implications

For governments, transport agencies, and AV developers: safety messaging alone is insufficient. Robust data protection standards, transparent privacy policies, and cybersecurity certification must be front and centre in any AV rollout strategy.

🌏 Disaster Economics Urban Resilience 🆕 April 2026

Earthquake Disaster and Economic Impact Assessment towards Sustainable Cities and Communities

Eko Agus Prasetio, Yukni Arifianti, Fitriani Agustin, Prahara Iqbal, Sarah Ismullah, Pandu Adi Minarno, Ikhwanut Taqwa, Ahmad Zulkifli, Nadiefa Hasna Shabiya

Sustainable Cities and Society: Advances · Elsevier

Indonesia is one of the most seismically active countries on Earth. Yet for Jakarta — a dense urban hub packed with high-value business assets — the economic cost of a major earthquake had never been rigorously mapped. This study closes that gap with a dual-framework approach combining seismic hazard assessment and economic impact modelling.
📐
The Methodology

The study integrates Peak Ground Acceleration (PGA) data, GIS-based fuzzy logic, geological conditions, and longitudinal GRDP data across 214 business facilities in Kebayoran Baru — one of South Jakarta's most commercially dense sub-districts.

⚠️
The Risk Overlap

High seismic hazard zones directly overlap clusters of high-value assets in finance, IT, legal services, and retail — yielding risk index values up to 0.704. These sectors sit in the most vulnerable locations.

💡
The Surprising Finding

Despite high risk scores, service-oriented and digital businesses show strong resilience. Flexible work models and cloud infrastructure mean disruption is largely limited to temporary access and equipment losses — not catastrophic collapse.

💰
The Cost Estimates

Reconstruction costs per building range from IDR 204.7 million to IDR 3.06 billion — providing a standardised, replicable framework for post-disaster financial planning for insurers, investors, and urban planners.

🏛️
Policy Implications

The study delivers actionable tools for decision-makers: risk maps to identify high-priority zones, cost models for budgeting, and targeted mitigation strategies — bridging the gap between scientific seismology and urban governance.

🌱
Broader Impact

The dual ERS–EIS framework offers a replicable blueprint for any emerging-economy city at the intersection of rapid urban growth and natural hazard exposure — well beyond Indonesia.

🚗 Autonomous Vehicles Market Segmentation Scopus Q1 · 2025

Cluster Analysis of Potential Autonomous Vehicle (AV) Adopters in Indonesia's New Capital

Eko Agus Prasetio, Dita Novizayanti, Aghnia Nadhira Aliya Putri

Transportation Research Interdisciplinary Perspectives · Elsevier

Indonesia's major cities are crippled by severe traffic congestion — causing air pollution, lost productivity, higher accident rates, and contributions to global warming. Autonomous Vehicles (AVs) promise to change this. But will Indonesians actually adopt them? This study maps who will adopt AVs first — and what it takes to reach each group.
🏙️
Why Nusantara?

Nusantara — Indonesia's new capital city — is uniquely positioned as the first region where AVs will be deployed. Civil servants relocating there form a captive, definable market, making it the ideal testing ground for AV adoption research.

📊
The Research Design

A survey of 356 participants addressed 13 factors relevant to AV adoption — from safety perceptions to hedonic motivation. Cluster analysis grouped respondents into four distinct adopter profiles.

👥
The Four Clusters

Cluster 1 (27%) — values sustainability & performance but needs trust assurances.
Cluster 2 (16%) — driven by enjoyment & emotional safety, low media influence.
Cluster 3 (38%) — focuses on usefulness & safety, largely ignores media.
Cluster 4 (18%) — values enjoyment & usefulness, least influenced by external factors.

🎯
The Strategic Insight

No single message works for all adopters. Each cluster has distinct barriers and motivators — requiring staged, targeted strategies: build trust first, then enhance positive perceptions, leverage social influence, and finally sustain enthusiasm with incentives.

🔒
The Safety Question

Across all four clusters, emotional safety is the single most consistent driver of adoption intent — more than performance, cost, or sustainability. Any AV rollout strategy must lead with safety communication.

🌏
Broader Significance

Indonesia's AV adoption dynamics reflect the broader challenge for emerging economies introducing frontier mobility technology. The cluster-based framework offers a replicable model for other developing nations planning smart city transitions.

Dari laboratorium menuju pasar: kasus-kasus nyata inovasi Indonesia

The path from lab to market is littered with institutional barriers, regulatory gaps, financing walls, and ecosystem failures. This book maps those challenges honestly — drawing on academic frameworks and firsthand experience inside ITB's technology transfer apparatus.

Tradisi Santri untuk Inovasi Imahnoong dan Pasar Purnama

Agus S. Ekomadyo, Eko Agus Prasetio, dkk.  ·  Kata Pengantar: K.H. Abdul Ghaffar Rozin & Hendro Setyanto

When Imahnoong — a community observatory in Kampung Areng, Lembang — began empowering local farmers, dairy herders, and village residents through astronomy-based ecotourism and the lunar Pasar Purnama market, it crossed a threshold: from a simple artefact of an idea into a living form of social innovation. 🔭
What is Imahnoong?

An accessible community observatory founded by astronomer Hendro Setyanto in Kampung Areng, Wangunsari Village, Lembang, Bandung. Designed to bring astronomy to anyone with curiosity — it grew from a telescope facility into a full platform for village economic empowerment.

💡
Social Innovation Defined

The book grounds Imahnoong in social innovation theory: innovations that solve social and environmental problems while forming new social relationships in the process. The "sweet spot" emerges when a new idea meets the founder's genuine competence — here, astronomy meets community building.

🌕
Pasar Purnama

A monthly lunar market co-created by Imahnoong founders and ITB researchers — giving Kampung Areng residents direct economic benefit. It is sustaining innovation: continuously improving, evolving year on year, and grounded in gotong-royong (mutual cooperation).

Open Innovation in Practice

Imahnoong demonstrates open innovation (Chesbrough, 2006) in a social context — drawing on talent and resources from Hendro Setyanto's team, ITB researchers, the ITB Alumni Association, a local pesantren, and village residents to co-create social value beyond what any single party could achieve alone.

🔄
The Virtuous Cycle

As economic, social, and knowledge benefits grow and community ownership deepens, Imahnoong becomes a social asset. This social capital feeds the next round of innovation — creating what the book calls a virtuous cycle: value co-creation that continuously regenerates itself.

🇮🇩
Indonesia's Local Wisdom

The book highlights how the process honours distinctly Indonesian values: musyawarah (deliberation), ngawangkong (informal community dialogue), and gotong-royong (collective effort) — proving that world-class social innovation can emerge from local roots.

How Indonesian digital startups manage learning to create innovation

🏦 Digital Transformation Banking Performance Scopus Q1 · 2024

Impact of Dynamic Capabilities on Digital Transformation and Innovation to Improve Banking Performance: A TOE Framework Study

Abdurrahman Abdurrahman, Aurik Gustomo, Eko Agus Prasetio

Journal of Open Innovation: Technology, Market, and Complexity · Vol. 10, 100215 · Elsevier · 2024

How do Indonesian banks successfully transform digitally? This study proposes that the answer lies in five distinct organisational capabilities — mapped to technology, organisation, and environment — and tests this model empirically with 325 respondents across 48 banks. The findings reveal unexpected tensions: some capabilities accelerate innovation while simultaneously constraining transformation, and vice versa.
🔬
The Research Gap

Despite growing research on Dynamic Capabilities (DC) and digital transformation, no study had empirically integrated DC with the Technology-Organisation-Environment (TOE) framework in banking DT and innovation. This study fills that gap.

🏗️
Five Capabilities Mapped

The model identifies five capabilities: Technology (IT management & integration), Organisational (structure, culture, agility), Strategy (digital vision & roadmap), Ecosystem (partner & customer networks), and GRC (Governance, Risk, Compliance).

📊
The Sample

325 respondents from 48 Indonesian banks — including state-owned, regional development, national private, Islamic, and conventional banks. Analysed using PLS-SEM.

💡
The Surprising Findings

Strategy capability strongly drives DT (+) but negatively impacts innovation (−). Organisational capability strongly drives innovation (+) but negatively impacts DT (−). Banks must manage these tensions deliberately.

🔗
Innovation as Mediator

Innovation partially mediates the relationship between all four organisational capabilities and digital transformation — with a consistent 50% Variance Accounted For (VAF) across all paths.

🎯
GRC as Moderator

GRC capability acts as a moderating variable — when included, bank performance (R² = 0.567) exceeds the model without it (R² = 0.555). Governance and compliance integration is a performance amplifier.
